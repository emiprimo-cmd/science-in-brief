Disordered Protein LAT Encodes the Balance of Signaling Pathways in T Cell Activation

A large-scale single-cell mutational screen reveals that the disordered adapter protein LAT encodes T cell signaling balance through distributed functional elements — not isolated residues.

psychology Context

When a T cell detects a foreign antigen, a rapid and precisely orchestrated cascade of molecular events is triggered. At the heart of this process sits LAT (Linker for Activation of T cells), a transmembrane adapter protein whose cytoplasmic tail is largely intrinsically disordered — meaning it lacks a stable three-dimensional structure. Upon T cell receptor (TCR) activation, LAT is phosphorylated by the kinase ZAP-70, and its disordered tail recruits protein partners to coordinate multiple downstream pathways simultaneously, including NFAT and AP-1.

The central puzzle has always been: how does a structurally disordered protein achieve such precise and balanced signal branching? Traditional biochemical approaches had identified a handful of key tyrosine residues, but a comprehensive, sequence-level understanding of how LAT encodes signaling balance was missing.

science What they did

Rubin et al. developed a pooled single-cell high-content screening approach to systematically dissect the sequence-function relationship of LAT. They generated a large library of LAT mutants — including triple alanine block substitutions covering the entire length of the protein, single and multisite variants, and charge-altering mutations — and introduced them into LAT-knockout Jurkat T cells via lentiviral transduction.

After TCR stimulation, individual cells were analyzed simultaneously at the epigenomic, transcriptomic, and cell-surface protein level using single-cell genomics. This allowed the authors to read out functional consequences of each LAT variant across multiple signaling dimensions in parallel and at single-cell resolution. Key results were validated in primary human CD4+ central memory T cells.

bolt Key finding

LAT contains numerous functional sequence elements — distributed across its disordered tail — that each influence multiple downstream signaling pathways in a coordinated manner. Rather than a small number of discrete on/off switches, signal balance is encoded through widespread functional regions, including protein interaction motifs and blocks of negative charge. The authors propose that LAT clustering, organized by multiple effector proteins acting as bridging molecules with dual roles, explains how signal branching is achieved.

rocket_launch Why it matters

This work reframes how we think about intrinsically disordered proteins (IDPs) in signaling. Instead of being passive scaffolds, disordered proteins like LAT may actively encode complex information — including the relative weighting of competing signaling outcomes — in a distributed, sequence-level manner. This has broad implications for understanding immune regulation and for designing therapeutic strategies that modulate T cell responses with greater precision, relevant to autoimmunity, cancer immunotherapy, and vaccine development.

warningLimitations

The primary screen was performed in Jurkat T cells, a leukemic cell line that does not fully recapitulate primary T cell biology. While findings were validated in primary human CD4+ T cells, the full functional landscape may differ in other subsets or activation contexts. The study also does not address how co-stimulatory signals or cytokine environment interact with LAT's intrinsic properties.

check_circleTake-home

LAT is not simply a passive docking platform. Its disordered sequence encodes the relative balance of T cell signaling outputs through distributed functional elements — a finding that challenges reductionist models of immune signal transduction.

Expert Insight

What makes this study particularly significant is the methodological leap: using single-cell multi-omics to read out functional consequences of hundreds of protein variants simultaneously. This deep mutational scan at single-cell resolution could become a broadly applicable framework for dissecting other disordered proteins in signaling contexts. The observation that blocks of negative charge contribute to function is also noteworthy — suggesting that electrostatic properties of disordered regions, not just specific interaction motifs, are biologically meaningful.
